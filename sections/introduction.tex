The study of the Bible has generated one of the largest and most enduring bodies of scholarly commentary in human history. Prominent theologians---from John Calvin in the sixteenth century to Archibald T. Robertson in the twentieth---have produced detailed, verse-by-verse analyses that illuminate the linguistic, historical, and theological dimensions of the biblical text. For the serious student, these commentaries are indispensable. Yet the sheer volume of material presents a practical challenge: consulting even a handful of commentators for a single verse requires navigating multiple lengthy texts, synthesizing sometimes divergent perspectives, and extracting the most relevant insights.

BibleGoAI grew out of a simple goal: \textit{help people study the Bible using time-tested commentaries presented in a simple way that even new believers can use}. The project was initiated and guided by a group of Christians---mostly living in the Cleveland, Ohio area---who have been ministering to churches for years. Through weekly round table meetings, this group of volunteers and full-time ministry leaders brainstormed how artificial intelligence could make quality biblical commentary more accessible, studied the needs of modern Bible readers, and analyzed existing platforms such as BibleHub, BlueLetterBible, and StudyLight to understand how commentary is currently delivered online. The original commentary texts were sourced from StudyLight.org.

Modern advances in natural language processing (NLP) and large language models (LLMs) offer new possibilities for addressing this challenge. Systems based on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) have demonstrated the ability to ground LLM outputs in authoritative source documents \citep{lewis2020rag}, while sentence embeddings \citep{cer2018use, reimers2019sentencebert} enable fine-grained semantic comparison between generated and source texts. These capabilities make it feasible to build systems that not only summarize but also \textit{validate} the fidelity of their outputs against the original material.

In this paper, we present \textbf{BibleGoAI}, a multi-stage pipeline that transforms curated biblical commentary into concise, source-adherent summary paragraphs called \textit{Brief Helpful Texts} (BHTs). The name reflects the project's core value: bringing the richness of difficult-to-read commentary to the fingertips of Bible readers in an accessible way. The system is designed around three principles:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Fidelity}: BHTs must be grounded in the actual words of trusted commentators, not in the LLM's own knowledge. At least 30\% of each BHT's words must originate from source quotes (with a target of 70\% and an upper bound of 90\%).
    \item \textbf{Conciseness}: Each BHT is constrained to 30--80 words in 1--3 sentences, making it practical for quick reference during Bible study.
    \item \textbf{Traceability}: Every claim in a BHT is linked back to its source commentary via footnotes with similarity scores, enabling readers to verify and explore further.
\end{enumerate}

The system has been applied to the entire New Testament (7{,}957 verses across 27 books) and the full Old Testament (all 39 books), served via two pipelines: a single-pass regular pipeline for 24 books and a two-stage split pipeline for 20 books (see Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} for details). It draws from a curated corpus of 14 commentators spanning four centuries and organized into three tiers of scholarly importance. The NT was publicly launched at a New Year's church conference in December 2023, with the OT following at a Memorial Day church conference in 2024. The resulting BHTs are deployed and actively served through \textbf{BibleGo.org}, a web application that provides interactive commentary exploration, original-language word study via Strong's concordance, and full commentator browsing, serving approximately 100--300 unique users per month with monthly page-view volumes ranging from ${\sim}200$ to ${\sim}2{,}500$ clicks depending on seasonal and community activity.

Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item A multi-stage pipeline for commentary synthesis combining LLM-based quote extraction, tiered prompt engineering, and constraint-enforced generation.
    \item A composite quality scoring framework that integrates semantic similarity, readability metrics, and source fidelity checks.
    \item An OT-specific two-pipeline design---a single-pass regular pipeline and a two-stage split pipeline---that together covers all 39 Old Testament books, with per-pipeline metrics reported across the deployed corpus.
    \item A deployed, user-facing application demonstrating the practical impact of the system.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{BibleGoAI} is a commentary-synthesis pipeline that automatically distills multi-author biblical commentary into concise, source-adherent summary paragraphs (called \textit{Brief Helpful Texts}, or BHTs) from a curated corpus of fourteen commentators spanning four centuries. The pipeline was run once over the full Bible to produce a fixed BHT corpus; each BHT is a short paragraph of roughly 80 words (see Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} for per-pipeline distributions), with automated quality scoring and provenance tracking via footnotes linking every claim back to its source commentary. The pipeline combines large language models (LLMs), retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and semantic similarity scoring across a multi-stage flow: quote extraction, tiered synthesis, constraint-enforced generation with best-of-five selection, and cross-encoder footnote linking. We evaluate the generated corpus across the full 27-book New Testament and the full 39-book Old Testament, comparing a single-pass regular pipeline against a two-stage split pipeline for OT books. The resulting BHTs and their source commentaries are served to end users through BibleGo.org (a web application providing interactive commentary exploration, original-language word study, and full commentator browsing), reaching approximately 100--300 unique users per month. Beyond the engineering contribution, BibleGoAI is also a record of a volunteer-driven faith-community collaboration: commentator curation by ministry leaders, a 28-volunteer digitization effort of Henry Alford's 2{,}544-page \textit{New Testament for English Readers} commentary, and iterative usability testing with the project's Bible study community.

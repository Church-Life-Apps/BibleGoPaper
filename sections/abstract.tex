\textbf{BibleGoAI} is a commentary-synthesis pipeline that automatically distills multi-author biblical commentary into concise, source-adherent summary paragraphs---called \textit{Brief Helpful Texts} (BHTs)---from a curated corpus of fourteen commentators spanning four centuries. The pipeline was run once over the full Bible to produce a fixed BHT corpus; each BHT targets a 30--80 word paragraph (observed means 63--84 words across the three generation variants reported in this paper), with automated quality scoring and provenance tracking via footnotes linking every claim back to its source commentary. The pipeline combines large language models (LLMs), retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and semantic similarity scoring across a multi-stage flow: quote extraction, tiered synthesis, constraint-enforced generation with best-of-five selection, and cross-encoder footnote linking. We evaluate the generated corpus across 7{,}957 New Testament verses and the full 39-book Old Testament, comparing a single-pass regular pipeline against a two-stage split pipeline for OT books. The resulting BHTs are served to end users through BibleGo.org---a web application providing interactive commentary exploration, original-language word study, and full commentator browsing---reaching approximately 100--300 unique users per month. Beyond the engineering contribution, BibleGoAI is also a record of a volunteer-driven faith-community collaboration: commentator curation by ministry leaders, a 28-volunteer digitization effort of Henry Alford's 2{,}544-page New Testament commentary, and iterative usability testing with the project's Bible study community.

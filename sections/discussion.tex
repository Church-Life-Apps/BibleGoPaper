\paragraph{Strengths.}
The multi-stage pipeline design offers several advantages over end-to-end generation. By extracting verbatim quotes before synthesis, the system creates an auditable intermediate representation that can be inspected and validated independently of the final BHT. The tiered commentator system ensures that the most authoritative voices dominate the output, while the constraint enforcement (word count, quote proportion, forbidden tokens) prevents many common failure modes of unconstrained LLM generation.

The iterative prompt refinement process---spanning over 20 prompt versions across three prompt families---proved essential. Early prompt versions (v0.1--v0.3) produced BHTs that frequently exceeded word limits, included commentator names, or adopted list formatting. By v0.8 (BHT) and v2.2 (choicest), these issues were largely eliminated, demonstrating the value of systematic prompt engineering with quantitative feedback loops.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations warrant discussion. First, the entire deployed corpus was generated using GPT-3.5-turbo, which was among the most capable generally available models at the time of development. A small, informal comparison on 13 verses against Llama~3.3-70B (via Groq) and GPT-4o-mini suggested broadly comparable quality, but the sample is too small to support general claims about model impact, and a full-scale regeneration and re-evaluation on newer models remains future work. Second, the quality scoring framework, while multi-dimensional, relies heavily on embedding-based similarity, which may not capture all aspects of theological accuracy or interpretive source adherence. Third, the 50\% quote proportion constraint, while ensuring grounding, can sometimes produce BHTs that read as stitched-together phrases rather than naturally flowing prose. Fourth, we do not yet include a zero-shot LLM baseline---e.g., prompting a frontier model directly with all available commentary for a verse and asking it to summarize---so the marginal value of the multi-stage pipeline over modern end-to-end generation has not been quantitatively isolated.

\paragraph{Source adherence vs.\ fluency.}
A fundamental tension exists between source adherence and fluency of the generated text. Higher quote proportions ensure adherence but can reduce coherence; lower proportions allow smoother prose but risk introducing LLM-generated content that may not reflect the commentators' actual positions. Our constraint of 50\% represents a pragmatic balance, validated through iterative testing and user feedback.

\paragraph{Prompt engineering insights.}
The evolution of prompts yielded several practical insights: (1)~explicit negative constraints (``do not mention commentators'') were more effective than positive-only instructions; (2)~specifying exact word count ranges reduced variance more than vague length instructions; (3)~the tiering language (``primarily,'' ``sparingly,'' ``minimally'') meaningfully influenced the tier distribution in generated outputs; and (4)~requesting numbered quotes in the extraction phase significantly improved downstream validation.

\paragraph{Project process.}
Several practical lessons emerged from the collaborative development process. The team's request to use the English Standard Version (ESV) was denied by the publisher, leading to the selection of the NASB 1995 with permission from the Lockman Foundation---a decision that proved beneficial given the NASB's reputation for literal translation. Over 18 demo presentations were prepared during an intensive April--May 2024 development period, each showcasing incremental improvements in generation quality, retrieval strategies, and UI features. These presentations, preserved in the project's demo archive, provided a structured mechanism for gathering feedback and tracking progress through the weekly round table meetings.

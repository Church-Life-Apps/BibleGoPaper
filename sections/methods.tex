The BibleGoAI pipeline transforms raw multi-author biblical commentary into concise, source-adherent summary paragraphs called \textit{Brief Helpful Texts} (BHTs). The process unfolds across six sequential phases, each building on the output of the previous. Figure~\ref{fig:bhtflow} illustrates the generation flow.

\subsection{Phase 1: Quote Extraction}

Given a target verse, the system loads all available commentary from the curated corpus. Each commentator's text is submitted to GPT-3.5-turbo \citep{openai2023gpt35} with a \textit{choicest prompt} (iteratively refined from v0.1 to v2.2) that instructs the model to extract one to three ``striking, unique, and enlightening'' quotes. The prompt enforces strict constraints:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item Quotes must be \textbf{verbatim}---no paraphrasing or injected words.
    \item Each quote must not exceed 100 words.
    \item Quotes should capture the most distinctive insights, avoiding generic observations.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Extracted quotes are validated against the original commentary to ensure fidelity. Quotes containing words absent from the source material are flagged and re-extracted. This phase produces a \texttt{ChoicestQuotesForVerse} object mapping each commentator to their selected excerpts.

\subsection{Phase 2: Retrieval}

As an alternative or supplement to direct quote extraction, the system supports a retrieval-augmented approach using dense vector search. Commentary texts are split into chunks using one of three strategies:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Character splitting}: Fixed-size chunks with overlap (via LangChain's \texttt{CharacterTextSplitter}) \citep{chase2022langchain}.
    \item \textbf{Recursive splitting}: Hierarchical splitting at paragraph, sentence, and word boundaries (\texttt{RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter}).
    \item \textbf{Semantic splitting}: Embedding-aware chunking that respects topical boundaries (\texttt{SemanticChunker}).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Chunks are embedded using HuggingFace's \texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2} sentence transformer \citep{song2020mpnet} and indexed with FAISS \citep{johnson2019faiss}. At query time, the target Bible verse is embedded and the top-$k$ most semantically similar commentary passages are retrieved, ranked by cosine similarity.

This retrieval mechanism enables coverage of commentaries that span multiple verses, where a single verse may not have a dedicated entry. Four retrieval variants were evaluated: simple character-based RAG, recursive RAG (R-RAG), semantic RAG (S-RAG), and a tagging-based approach (TAG) that uses LLM-driven verse segmentation.

\subsection{Phase 3: BHT Generation}

The core synthesis step produces a BHT from the extracted or retrieved quotes. Commentators are organized into three tiers (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}), and the selected quotes are submitted to GPT-3.5-turbo with a \textit{BHT prompt} (v0.1--v0.8) specifying the following constraints:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item The output must be a single coherent paragraph of 30--70 words in 1--3 sentences.
    \item At least 50\% of the words must originate from the provided quotes.
    \item The paragraph should draw primarily from Tier~1, sparingly from Tier~2, and minimally from Tier~3.
    \item No references to commentators by name, verse numbers, or the word ``passage.''
    \item The text must not take the form of a list.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The system makes up to five generation attempts per verse, scoring each candidate and retaining the highest-quality result.

\subsection{Phase 4: Split-BHT Variant}

To explore whether multi-stage generation improves output quality, a \textit{split-BHT} pipeline decomposes generation into two sub-steps:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Split-A}: An initial prompt produces a draft synthesis focused on extracting key themes from the tiered quotes.
    \item \textbf{Split-B}: A refinement prompt polishes the Split-A output into a final BHT, enforcing word count, quote proportion, and stylistic constraints.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Both sub-prompts were independently versioned and evaluated. This approach trades additional API calls for potentially higher coherence and fidelity.

\subsection{Phase 5: Scoring and Validation}

Every generated BHT undergoes a multi-dimensional quality assessment combining hard constraints with continuous metrics.

\paragraph{Hard constraints.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item Word count within 30--80 (strict range: 20--100).
    \item Quote token proportion between 30\% and 90\%.
    \item Absence of forbidden tokens (commentator names, verse references, ``passage'').
    \item No list formatting.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Continuous metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Commentary accuracy}: Tier-weighted cosine similarity between the BHT and source quotes, computed via the Universal Sentence Encoder \citep{cer2018use}. A minimum score of 2.3 is required.
    \item \textbf{Verse accuracy}: Cosine similarity between the BHT and the target verse embedding, measuring topical relevance.
    \item \textbf{Readability}: Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level, Flesch Reading Ease, Gunning Fog Index, Automated Readability Index, and Dale-Chall score \citep{kincaid1975readability, gunning1952fog}.
    \item \textbf{Tier distribution}: The proportion of BHT content attributable to each commentator tier, measured via per-tier semantic similarity.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Among the up to five generation attempts, the BHT with the highest composite quality score is selected. The scoring function prioritizes commentary accuracy while penalizing outputs that violate hard constraints.

\subsection{Phase 6: Footnoting and Tagging}

The final phase establishes provenance links between the generated BHT and its source commentaries.

\paragraph{Footnoting.} Each sentence in the BHT is compared against all source quotes using cross-encoder scoring via \texttt{sentence-transformers} \citep{reimers2019sentencebert}. For each sentence, the system identifies the most similar quote and generates a footnote containing the commentator name, quote index, similarity score, and source location. This enables readers to trace any claim back to its originating commentary.

\paragraph{Commentary tagging.} For commentaries spanning multiple verses, a \textit{tag commentary prompt} (v0.1--v0.7) instructs GPT to segment the text into per-verse sections. The output is a JSON structure mapping each verse number to its relevant content, enabling verse-level retrieval from range-based sources.

\subsection{The OT Challenge}
\label{sec:otchallenge}

The New Testament pipeline described above was designed for commentary that is organized verse-by-verse, which is the norm for NT commentators. After the public release of NT BHTs, the team turned to the Old Testament---and encountered a fundamental structural difference: OT commentary is rarely organized by individual verse. Instead, commentators typically discuss passages spanning multiple verses, making direct quote extraction for a single verse impractical.

Two initial approaches were explored:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Commentary ``versification''}: Using GPT to tag multi-verse commentary with per-verse labels, effectively segmenting it into verse-level sections before applying the standard NT pipeline. While conceptually appealing, the accuracy of automated verse tagging proved inconsistent at scale.
    \item \textbf{Enhanced choicest extraction}: Modifying the choicest prompt to extract verse-specific quotes directly from multi-verse commentary, bypassing the need for segmentation. This also proved unreliable, as the model struggled to isolate verse-relevant content from longer passages.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Both approaches were evaluated over several weeks. The breakthrough came when an AI/ML engineer suggested using retrieval-augmented generation: rather than requiring verse-level segmentation, the system could embed commentary chunks and retrieve the most semantically relevant passages for each verse via vector search. This RAG-based approach (Phase~2) produced satisfactory results and unlocked OT BHT generation at scale, ultimately enabling completion of the full Bible corpus.
